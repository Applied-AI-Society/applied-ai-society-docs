# Student / Explorer Playbook

This playbook is for anyone who is still finding their way. That might mean you are 21 and about to graduate. It might mean you are 45 and starting over. Being a student is a mindset, not an age. If you are curious, hungry, and willing to learn by doing, this is for you.

## The Two Paths

When it comes to applied AI, you probably fall into one of two categories:

### Path 1: You Have a Burning Domain Calling

You see a specific industry or problem with absolute clarity. You know the pain points because you have lived them. You have a vision for how AI should transform that space, and you cannot stop thinking about it.

This is rare. If this is you, go build. But be honest with yourself about whether you truly have domain expertise or whether you just think the idea sounds cool. The difference matters.

### Path 2: You Want to Be Useful (Most People)

You are smart, capable, and excited about AI. But you do not have a specific industry burning in your chest. You are looking for where you fit.

This is most people. And it is a great position to be in.

Here is the honest advice: **do not try to pull a company idea out of thin air for an industry you do not deeply understand.** You would be competing with people who have years of reference experience on the actual issues and opportunities within that industry. The tools they already use, the workflows they already run, the problems they actually have. Unless you have a genuine edge, the odds are not in your favor.

Instead: **become invaluable to someone who already has traction.**

## The Applied AI Practitioner Path

The most valuable thing you can do right now is learn how to apply AI to real problems for real people. Not in theory. In practice.

Here is why this works:

1. **Relationships are the bottleneck.** The biggest AI companies on earth (OpenAI, Anthropic, xAI) are all building consulting arms because businesses cannot figure out how to use AI on their own. But those companies cannot scale trust. Trust is local, relational, and earned one conversation at a time. You can compete on trust.

2. **You do not need to be a domain expert.** You need to be good at listening, understanding someone's situation, and figuring out where AI fits. The domain expert is your client. You are the translator between their world and the AI world.

3. **You can start small.** Help one person with one problem. A local business owner who needs their invoicing automated. A professor who wants to use AI in their research workflow. A nonprofit that needs better donor outreach. Start with anyone who will let you help.

4. **Every engagement teaches you.** Each person you help gives you a deeper understanding of how businesses actually work, what the real pain points are, and where AI creates the most value. This knowledge compounds fast.

5. **You can do this solo.** You do not need a company, a team, or funding. You need a laptop, curiosity, and the willingness to sit down with someone and figure out their problems.

## What to Do This Week

1. **Pick one person in your life who runs a business or a team.** Could be a family member, a professor, a local shop owner, anyone.

2. **Ask them: "What takes up the most time in your day that you wish you could automate or speed up?"** Listen carefully. Do not pitch. Just listen.

3. **Go home and figure out if AI can help with that problem.** Use Claude, ChatGPT, or whatever tool you like. Build a quick prototype or write up a proposal for how it could work.

4. **Show it to them.** Not a pitch deck. A working thing, or at least a clear explanation of how it would work. Let them react.

5. **Iterate.** If they are interested, keep going. If not, try someone else. The goal is reps.

## What to Learn

You do not need to learn everything. Focus on:

- **How to talk to non-technical people about AI.** This is the most valuable skill. Most people cannot have a productive conversation about AI because no one has taught them how. Be the person who can.
- **How to scope a pilot.** What is the specific problem? What does success look like? How long will it take? What does it cost? These are the questions that turn a vague "we should use AI" into an actual project.
- **How to use AI tools effectively.** Not how they work under the hood (unless you are interested). How to use them to get real work done. Prompting, context engineering, building workflows.
- **How businesses actually work.** Invoicing, scheduling, customer outreach, hiring, operations. The more you understand about how businesses run, the better you can spot where AI fits.

## The Bigger Picture

Applied AI is the new coding. Learning how to apply AI to grow businesses is like learning how to code was 10 years ago. The demand is infinite. The tools are accessible. And the window is wide open.

The people who start now, even from zero, are going to be in an incredibly strong position in 12 to 24 months. Not because they picked the right startup idea. Because they built the skill of helping real people with real problems using AI. That skill does not go out of style.
